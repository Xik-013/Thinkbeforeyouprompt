Anthropic's pledge — May 2026
Covering the cost — a new accountability model
Anthropic became the first pure-play AI company to commit to paying 100% of grid upgrade costs required to connect its data centers, investing in curtailment systems to cut power use during grid stress, and deploying water-efficient cooling technologies. It also committed to bringing new power generation online to match its demand rather than displacing existing consumers. OpenAI made a parallel pledge to "pay its own way" on electricity costs. Whether these commitments hold as Stargate scales from 10 GW to 250 GW remains the defining accountability question of the decade.
Future projections & risks

What happens next
if nothing changes

Current trajectories point to a collision between AI infrastructure demands and planetary limits — across energy grids, freshwater reserves, and climate commitments. These are not distant scenarios; several are already underway.

Near-term · 2025–2027
Grid destabilisation & blackout risk
US data center demand is projected to surpass 250 TWh by 2026 and reach 325–580 TWh by 2028. Local grids in Virginia, Texas, and Arizona are already strained. AI inference workloads run 24/7, creating relentless baseload with no slack for heatwaves or plant outages. Ireland and parts of the Netherlands have imposed moratoriums on new data center grid connections. Virginia's data centers already consume 26% of the state's total electricity. 250+ TWh US demand by 2026 Brookings Institution
Near-term · 2025–2028
Freshwater stress in arid regions
Hyperscale data centers are disproportionately built in water-scarce areas. In Arizona, data centers withdrew massive amounts during 2023 while farmers fallowed fields. Google's The Dalles, Oregon facility nearly tripled water use from 2017 to 2022 — one Iowa data center alone consumed 1 billion gallons in 2024, enough to supply all of Iowa's residential water for five days. Direct hyperscale water use is projected to reach 16–33 billion gallons annually by 2028. 1B gal — one Iowa site in 2024 Fast Company / Rutgers Law
Medium-term · 2026–2030
Climate pledges becoming untenable
Microsoft's emissions are already ~30% higher than 2020, threatening its 2030 carbon-negative target. Meta's 2024 emissions were 70% above 2019 levels. Google's surged nearly 50% since 2019. All four companies claim 100% renewable matching, but only 66% of Google's actual hourly consumption was served by carbon-free energy in 2024. A Guardian investigation found real emissions from company-owned data centers may be 7× higher than reported. 7× underreported emissions Food & Water Watch
Medium-term · 2026–2030
Energy cost pass-through to consumers
Power-hungry AI data centers are already raising electricity bills and blackout risk for households. As hyperscalers secure priority grid access and long-term PPAs, smaller consumers bear higher marginal costs. Data center AI electricity demand is expected to triple from 2025 to 2030, with a significant share met by fossil fuel plants kept open specifically for this sector. Coal remains the largest single source of data center electricity globally, at ~30% share. Coal: 30% of global DC power IEA — Energy & AI 2026
Long-term · 2028–2030
Nuclear deals won't deliver in time
Google, Microsoft, and Amazon have all signed nuclear power deals — SMRs and plant restarts — but none will deliver power before 2030. Solar and wind are the only sources that can scale before then. The IEA projects renewables will meet nearly half of additional demand by 2030, with natural gas and coal filling most of the rest. Even in its base case, Big Tech's clean energy commitments and actual carbon-free power availability diverge significantly through the rest of the decade. No new nuclear before 2030 IEA — Energy Supply for AI
Long-term · 2026–2030
Data centers as a country: bigger than Japan
At 950 TWh by 2030, global data center electricity demand will exceed Japan's entire current national consumption. Under a high-growth scenario, it could reach 1,050+ TWh — making it the fifth largest energy consumer on the planet, between Japan and Russia. The IEA's capital expenditure data is equally striking: five tech companies now invest more in data centers than the entire global oil and gas industry invests in production — and that capex is set to jump 75% in 2026. 950 TWh by 2030 = all of Japan IEA — Key Questions on Energy & AI
The compounding risk — all crises interact
A heatwave is also a data center crisis, a water crisis, and a climate setback — simultaneously
These are not isolated problems. A heatwave that strains electricity grids also forces data centers to ramp up water-cooled systems. Water stress forces a switch to air cooling, which raises energy use. Rising energy costs make renewable PPAs more expensive, slowing green transitions. Climate pledges slip, triggering regulatory action that raises operating costs, which get passed to consumers — who are also using more AI. The feedback loops between grid capacity, freshwater availability, climate goals, energy pricing, fossil fuel lock-in, and public accountability mean that failing to address any one thread accelerates the others. Each prompt sent, each image generated, each model trained adds one more increment to a system already under strain. The window for proactive correction — rather than reactive crisis management — is narrow, and it is closing. Sustainability Atlas
